Core approach

The small farm strategy

The biggest mistake small landowners make is planting commodity crops like corn or soybeans. You can't compete at scale. The leverage is going high-value and selling direct-to-consumer, which typically pays 2–3× more than wholesale.

01 — Market

Direct sales first

Farmers markets, CSA subscriptions, and restaurant relationships pay dramatically more than wholesale. Secure buyers before you scale production — the fastest way to lose money is growing without a market.

02 — Crops

High-value over volume

One intensively managed acre of garlic, salad greens, or cut flowers can outperform ten acres of soybeans. The most profitable small farms grow 3–5 complementary crops, not one.

03 — Soil

Start with a soil test

UK Extension does them for ~$10. Knowing your soil pH and nutrient levels saves you from wasting money on the wrong inputs and dramatically improves your first-year yields.

04 — Cash flow

Layer fast and slow crops

Pair quick-return crops like salad greens and flowers (income in weeks) with long-term investments like elderberry and chestnut that pay off for decades. Don't bet everything on one timeline.

Kentucky advantage

Central Kentucky is one of the best-watered, most fertile farming climates on the continent. You get 40–50 inches of rain per year, zone 6b winters, and long productive summers. The Lexington Farmers Market is well-established and has strong consumer demand for local produce.

Year 1 roadmap

Month 1–2
Observe & test
Get a soil test from UK Extension. Walk your land in different weather. Identify drainage patterns, sun exposure, and any existing perennials worth keeping. The best food forest planners spend a full year observing before planting trees.
Month 2–3
Find your market first
Visit the Lexington Farmers Market. Talk to vendors about what sells. Contact local restaurants about direct purchasing. Apply for a vendor spot — waiting lists can be long.
Month 3–4
Apply for funding
Kentucky State University offers grants up to $5,000 for small farm equipment. The Kentucky Agricultural Development Fund backs organic production, agroforestry, and value-added processing. Apply early — these have deadlines.
Spring, Year 1
Plant your fast crops
Salad greens, radishes, herbs, and cut flowers can generate income within 6–8 weeks. Start a CSA with 10–15 subscribers at $25–30/week. This covers your operating costs while you build the longer-term income base.
Fall, Year 1
Plant garlic & elderberry
Garlic goes in the ground in fall and comes out the following July. Elderberry establishes fast and starts producing in year 2–3. These are your first perennial income investments.

Permaculture system

One-acre food forest plan

Kentucky zone 6b is ideal for a food forest — one of the most well-watered, fertile climates on the continent. A mature food forest is the lowest-labor income on your property, but it requires patience. Expect partial harvests by year 3, meaningful income by year 6–10.

Layer 1 — Canopy (30–50 ft)
Chestnut, Black Walnut, Pecan, Persimmon
Chinese/hybrid chestnut is the top pick — disease-resistant, prolific, and in high demand. Black walnut is native to Kentucky and sells for both nuts and timber. One mature chestnut can produce 500 lbs of nuts annually.
$4–$8.50/lb conventional · $7–$12.50/lb organic
Layer 2 — Sub-canopy (15–30 ft)
Pawpaw, Heirloom Apple, Asian Pear, Plum, Peach
Pawpaw is a Kentucky native (KSU hosts the national USDA pawpaw research repository in Frankfort) with explosive demand from chefs, breweries, and specialty markets. Heirloom apples and Asian pears command premium direct-to-consumer prices.
Pawpaw: ~$2,200 returns/acre · strong value-added potential (ice cream, beer, syrup)
Layer 3 — Shrub (5–15 ft)
Elderberry, Hazelnut, Goumi, Aronia, Currants
Elderberry is the star for Kentucky — fast-producing, very low maintenance, and in explosive demand for syrups and immune supplements. Established plantings yield 5,000–8,000 lbs/acre. Hazelnuts produce for 30–40 years with minimal inputs.
Elderberry: $3–$5.50/lb bulk · dried $20–$31/lb · Hazelnut: $3,000–$4,000/acre/yr
Layer 4 — Herbaceous (1–5 ft)
Comfrey, Rhubarb, Walking Onions, Lemon Balm, Echinacea
These fill gaps and serve double duty — comfrey fixes nitrogen and can be sold as a soil amendment, herbs sell at markets, rhubarb is a fast first-year earner before trees establish.
Culinary/medicinal herbs: $10K+/acre equivalent in dense planting
Layer 5 — Ground cover
Strawberries, Clover, Creeping Thyme, Mint
Strawberries between rows earn income while trees mature. White clover feeds pollinators and fixes nitrogen. Creeping thyme suppresses weeds and doubles as a harvestable culinary herb.
Strawberries direct-to-consumer: $4.66–$6.30/lb (2024 data)
Layer 6 — Vine (climbing)
Grapes, Blackberries, Raspberries, Hops, Hardy Kiwi
Train up fence lines and tree trunks. Grapes and blackberries are the most commercially viable in Kentucky. Hops serve local craft breweries. Hardy kiwi is an under-the-radar high-value crop for niche markets.
Blackberries/raspberries: $4–$7/pint at market · strong CSA and restaurant demand
Layer 7 — Root / Fungal
Ramps (Wild Leeks), Goldenseal, Shiitake on Logs, Black Cohosh
The highest-value hidden layer. Shade-grown medicinals like goldenseal and black cohosh are native to Kentucky and sell to herbal supplement companies. Shiitake on hardwood logs thrives in forest shade and has very short crop cycles.
Goldenseal: $30–$50/lb dried root · Shiitake: ~$12/lb with fast turnaround
Food forest vs. market garden: how to use both

Run the food forest alongside 1–2 acres of market garden crops (garlic, greens, flowers) that pay the bills while trees mature. Think of the food forest as building a long-term asset — by year 10, a well-planted acre in Kentucky could generate mostly passive income with very little annual labor.

Can you hit $25,000/year on 3.1 income acres?

Yes — 1 acre of intensive market garden (salad greens, garlic, tomatoes) can gross $15K–$30K direct-to-consumer. Add elderberry on another acre ($8K–$21K at maturity), and a productive greenhouse alone can generate $10K+ in microgreens or seedlings. The math works even with just 1–2 acres in active production — the remaining land is your food forest investment building long-term value.

Suggested layout by zone

Zone A — Near house

Home & infrastructure

House, barn, greenhouse, and chicken coop grouped together for ease of daily management. South-facing greenhouse placement maximizes light. Chickens near barn for feed storage convenience.

Zone B — ¼–½ acre

Intensive market garden

1 acre of intensively managed beds for salad greens, garlic, tomatoes, cut flowers. Irrigation runs from a central header. This is your primary year 1–5 income generator.

Zone C — ½–1½ acres

Elderberry & perennials

Elderberry rows, lavender, herbs. Lower maintenance than the market garden, begins producing by year 2–3. Sets up your mid-term income as the market garden work intensifies.

Zone D — Outer acre

Food forest

Chestnuts, pawpaws, hazelnuts, with understory of elderberry, currants, and medicinals. Low annual labor once established. The 10–20 year investment that generates passive income.

Kentucky-specific

Local resources & funding

These are often underutilized. Kentucky has stronger small-farm infrastructure than most states — take advantage of it before spending your own money.

KSU Small Scale Farm Grants

Kentucky State University offers grants up to $5,000 for small farm equipment. Contact Joni Nelson at joni.nelson@kysu.edu or 502-597-6831. Apply early — funding is limited.

Kentucky Ag Development Fund

Backs practical upgrades in organic agriculture, agroforestry, aquaculture, beekeeping, value-added processing, and ag technology. Strong match for a food forest operation.

UK Cooperative Extension

Free local advice from farm agents in every county. Offers soil testing (~$10), workshops, crop guides, and one-on-one farm planning sessions. Use this first, before any paid consultant.

Kentucky Proud

State marketing program that connects local farmers to buyers and gives you a recognized brand label. Being "Kentucky Proud" certified can command a premium at markets and with restaurants.

KCARD

Kentucky Center for Agriculture and Rural Development. Helps write grant applications, connect with funding programs, and develop business plans for beginning farmers.

KSU Pawpaw Research

Kentucky State University in Frankfort hosts the USDA National Clonal Germplasm Repository for pawpaw. If you want to grow pawpaws, you're in the right state — contact them for variety recommendations and resources.

Midwest Elderberry Cooperative

Provides market access for elderberry growers who can't sell direct to end buyers. Certified organic American elderberries: $3.50–$5.50/lb. Sustainably grown: $3.00–$4.50/lb (2025 prices).

Lexington Farmers Market

Well-established market with strong consumer demand. Get on the vendor waiting list early. Visit as a customer first — talk to vendors about what sells and what margins look like in person.
